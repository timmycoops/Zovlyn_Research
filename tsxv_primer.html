The TSX Venture Exchange looks simple from the retail side — you see a price, a bid, an ask, and a "broker summary" table on Stockwatch showing who bought and sold. That view is a stripped, aggregated, and time-lagged projection of a far more complex reality: 14 competing trading venues, five tiers of data visibility, encrypted private order markers, broker preferencing rules that let dealers jump the queue, settlement netting that hides individual failures, and a regulatory feed nobody outside the exchange or CIRO ever sees. This primer walks through each layer in turn.

THE 14 CANADIAN TRADING VENUES

Every TSX and TSX.V-listed security can be traded across all 14 venues simultaneously. A single order placed by a retail investor may execute on TSX, Alpha, or any ATS — whichever offers the best price at that instant. The broker is obligated to achieve "best execution" across all venues. This fragmentation is why retail broker summary data often understates actual volume — many platforms only capture their primary venue.

THE COMPLETE ORDER LIFECYCLE

Every trade in a TSX or TSX.V-listed security passes through this chain from order entry to settlement. Each step creates opportunities for information asymmetry.

1
Order Entry — Client to Broker
Retail investor places order via app or phone. Broker's system tags the order with: long/short designation, anonymous flag (Y/N), SME flag (if applicable), order type (limit/market/iceberg/dark), DMA flag, duration. These private markers travel with the order through the system but are never visible to the public or counterparty. Only the broker and the exchange see them.
Anonymous: Y/N SME Flag: Y/N Short: Y/N
2
Smart Order Router (SOR) — Venue Selection
The broker's SOR algorithm scans all 14 venues in real time to find the best available price across the consolidated order book. It may split a 50,000-share order across TSX (30,000), Alpha (10,000), and MatchNow dark (10,000) simultaneously. The client sees one fill. The market sees fragmented prints across venues. This makes it impossible to reconstruct the full picture from any single venue's data feed — you need the consolidated feed (CDF).
Best execution obligation Multi-venue splitting
3
Broker Preferencing Check — Internal First
Before the order hits the open book on TSX/TSX.V, the matching engine checks: does this broker have contra-side orders at the same price that have time priority over other brokers? If yes — and the order is not marked anonymous — the order is matched internally (broker-preferenced) against its own client orders first. The trade executes at the same price but never competed with the open market. Only attributed (non-anonymous) orders can participate in broker preferencing.
Attributed orders only Anonymous orders skip this step
4
CLOB Matching — Price/Broker/Time Priority
If not preferenced, the order enters the Central Limit Order Book (CLOB). Matching priority on TSX/TSX.V is: (1) Best price, (2) Same broker has priority over other brokers at the same price, (3) Time. Dark orders and iceberg hidden portions execute after all visible same-price orders are filled. The exchange's matching engine runs at sub-100 microsecond latency (TMX Quantum XA). HFT firms co-located at the data centre have ~200 microsecond round-trip advantage over remote participants.
Price → Broker → Time <100μs execution Dark fills after visible
5
Trade Reporting — Public Feed vs. Private Feed
On execution, the trade is reported simultaneously to two feeds. The public CLS (Consolidated Last Sale) feed broadcasts: price, volume, venue, timestamp, and special markers (VWAP cross, basis cross, etc.) — but the anonymous flag, SME flag, and dark tag are stripped. The private TMX broadcast feed contains the full order detail including all private markers, encrypted. Only exchange participants and CIRO receive the private feed. This is the "Level 3" data point — it is what you would need to reconstruct who did what.
Public: price + volume Private: all markers encrypted
6
Clearing — CDS Continuous Net Settlement (CNS)
All exchange trades flow automatically into CDS (Canadian Depository for Securities, a TMX subsidiary). CDS runs CNS — it novates the trade (replaces broker-to-broker obligation with two broker-to-CDS obligations) and nets all of each broker's buy and sell activity in each security across the entire day into a single net position. A broker who bought 500K and sold 480K of a stock today has a single net CNS obligation to deliver 20K shares. This netting is why naked shorts can persist: individual failures are buried inside the net position and the aggregate may still settle.
Novation: bilateral → CDS-central Net position hides individual fails
7
Settlement — T+1 Delivery vs. Payment
Settlement occurs the next business day (T+1, since May 2024). CDS runs four intraday CNS cycles plus a real-time settlement process. At settlement, securities are delivered from seller's CDS depository account to buyer's, and cash moves in the opposite direction (DVP — Delivery vs. Payment). If a broker's depository account doesn't have enough shares, the position becomes a fail-to-deliver — it rolls to the next day and CIRO receives an Extended Failed Trade report if it persists.
T+1 since May 2024 FTD = potential naked short signal

CDS Depository — Settlement Layer (Separate from Trading Data)

CDS participant account data is not publicly available — accessible only to: the participant themselves, CDS, CIRO, and the securities regulators.

The naked short gap: Because CNS nets all positions, a broker who sold 500K shares they don't own (naked short) may still settle their net position if they also bought 600K legitimately that same day. The 500K naked short is invisible — it nets out. Only if the broker is a net fail-to-deliver does CIRO see a signal. This is why the CDS depository imbalance method (total client-reported holdings vs. actual depository holdings) is the only reliable detection method — it looks at the cumulative stock of uncovered shorts, not the daily flow.

WHAT THE PUBLIC TAPE HIDES

Every trade that appears in a public broker summary is a stripped, aggregated version of reality. Here is what is systematically removed before you see it.

Anonymous Flag
The real broker behind "001" is known to: the exchange, CIRO, and the broker themselves. Issuer can unmask it monthly via InfoSuite. Removed from every other public feed. Affects: ~30–40% of TSX.V trades on active names.
SME (Short-Marking Exempt) Flag
Orders marked SME do not appear in the public short interest data (CSPR). A market maker shorting 500K shares SME-flagged is invisible to every short interest report. Affects: all market maker and arbitrage activity.
Dark Order Tag
Trades that execute against dark orders show on the consolidated tape as normal prints — price, volume, venue. The fact that one or both sides was a dark order is stripped. Affects: all TSX DRK, MatchNow, Liquidnet executions.
Broker Preferencing Outcome
When two orders from the same broker match via preferencing, the trade appears identical on tape to any other trade. The fact that it jumped queue over time-priority orders from other dealers is not recorded in any public data. Affects: ~15–25% of TSX/TSX.V volume.
Iceberg Hidden Portion
An iceberg order displaying 5,000 shares may have 500,000 shares hidden. When it executes, the tape shows the execution volume but the hidden tip-to-total ratio is never revealed. Affects: all large passive accumulation programs.
Multi-Venue Fragmentation
A single 100,000-share order split across 6 venues generates 6 separate prints on 6 separate tapes. Without the consolidated CDF, the order appears to be 6 unrelated small trades from 6 different participants. Affects: all institutional VWAP programs.
OPACITY MECHANISMS — LEGAL TO ILLEGAL

A spectrum of tools and techniques that reduce transparency in the market. Most are legal and structural features of the exchange design; a few are outright illegal but persist because they are hard to detect from public data alone.

WHO IS IN THE MARKET AND WHAT THEY WANT

Seven distinct participant types compete on the TSX.V. They have different goals, time horizons, tool access, and information edges. Understanding who is across the tape from you is the first step to understanding why a price is moving.

Perspective: you are the broker. Your job is to maximise revenue for your firm. You have zero fiduciary duty to the issuer. The mechanisms below are all available to you — ranging from fully legal and structural, to grey-zone, to outright illegal. Understanding them from this side is the only way to recognise them when they appear in the tape.

Play 1 — The Placement Arbitrage
LEGALINFORMATION EDGE

You are running a flow-through or private placement at price $P. You know about this deal weeks before the market does. The stock is trading at roughly $P × 0.6. You accumulate for your favoured institutional clients before the announcement — anonymous VWAP orders through TSX DRK. Those clients get in at ~$P × 0.64. Placement subscribers later pay $P. On announcement the stock rerates. Your pre-placement clients are immediately 50%+ ahead; they become stickier clients and tell friends.

MECHANICS
  • Pre-deal window: accumulate anonymously via dark pool — no visible buyer in the book
  • Placement close: earn typical agent commission (5–7%) plus broker warrants
  • Favoured clients enter at pre-deal cost basis; placement at announced price
  • Revenue stack: commission + warrant value + client stickiness
Play 2 — Market Maker Spread + Directional Accumulation
LEGALSTRUCTURAL

You are the retained market maker — paid a small annual fee by the issuer to provide two-sided quotes. The SME (Short Marking Exempt) flag lets your orders bypass short sale reporting. You quote two-sided, capture the bid/ask spread on volume, and simultaneously accumulate a directional long position — which is invisible in short interest data and covered (nominally) by your market making obligations.

MECHANICS
  • Spread capture: ~20% of daily volume × typical spread × trading days
  • Directional build: SME-flagged so position doesn't show in CSPR short interest
  • On rerate: close the directional long at profit, resume pure market-making
Play 3 — Internalization / Broker Preferencing Cross
LEGAL

You are a large Schedule-I bank broker. You match your own institutional buyers against your own retail or post-hold sellers internally via broker preferencing — same price as the public book but without ever exposing the flow. Charge commission on both sides. Optionally insert yourself as principal for an extra cent of spread. The trade appears identical to any other trade on the public tape.

MECHANICS
  • Client A: institutional fund wants to buy
  • Client B: retail or post-hold seller wants out
  • Broker preferencing matches them internally — neither sees the counterparty
  • Double commission + optional principal spread
Play 4 — FT / PP Calendar Short
GREY ZONE

You ran a flow-through or private placement. You know the exact date the hold period expires and the cost basis of the subscribers. One to two weeks before expiry, you short anonymously. FT holders sell into the open market on expiry, price drops, you cover. The short has a "reasonable expectation to settle" because you know the supply is coming. Legal — but information you obtained as placement agent is the only reason you can time it this precisely.

MECHANICS
  • Short anonymously in the 1–2 weeks before hold expiry
  • Hold expiry: subscriber selling arrives, price drops
  • Cover at lower price; SME-flagged short never appears in CSPR
Play 5 — Hard-to-Borrow Stock Lending
LEGALPASSIVE INCOME

You lend your retail margin clients' shares to short sellers. Margin account terms (which almost no one reads) permit this. You keep the borrow fee. On an illiquid junior, hard-to-borrow rates can run 5–20% annually. Your own clients' shares are being used to bet against their own position — and the fee you collect is not shared with them.

MECHANICS
  • Retail client holds shares in a margin account
  • Shares automatically enrolled in lending pool unless client opts out in writing
  • Shares lent to short seller at annual borrow rate
  • Broker keeps 100% of fee; client receives nothing
Play 6 — End-of-Day Price Depression
GREY ZONEDOCUMENTED TSX.V

Sell anonymously in the final 5 minutes of trading to depress the closing price. The close is used for: margin calculations, fund NAVs, stop-loss triggers, covenant tests. Depressed close = forced sellers tomorrow morning = cheaper accumulation. Documented in detail by Terry Lynch (Power Nickel / PNPN) and the Generation IACP research team.

MECHANICS
  • 3:55pm: small anonymous sell at market depresses the close
  • Margin holders' ratios slightly worse → triggered sells next morning
  • Buy those forced shares at a lower average next day
  • Repeated over weeks: materially lower accumulation cost on multi-million share programs
Play 7 — VWAP Front-Running Your Own Client
ILLEGAL

Client gives you a large VWAP buy order. You know it will move the price in an illiquid stock. Prop desk buys ahead of the client's order. Price moves up. You sell the prop position back into your own client's demand. Client overpays. You capture the spread. This is front-running — a criminal offence under provincial securities legislation. Still happens, because enforcement requires proving intent.

WHY IT STILL HAPPENS
  • Compliance "information barriers" are a policy — enforcement requires proving intent
  • In small brokers, the "wall" is one person's ethics
  • CIRO surveillance flags egregious cases — misses subtle ones
  • Cross-broker version: tip a favoured client about a pending large buy. Same offence, harder to prove.
Play 8 — Naked Short + Coordinated Selling
ILLEGAL

Sell shares you don't own and can't actually borrow. The SME flag prevents CSPR disclosure. The position imbalance grows in CDS: broker depository holdings < client-reported holdings. Only detectable via the CDS depository imbalance method (Generation IACP). Power Nickel found 9M phantom shares — from 3M to 9M over 8 weeks — across the 80% of float they could monitor.

DETECTION METHOD
  • Transfer agent + broker CDS depository data: sum all broker depository holdings
  • Compare against total shares issued (SEDAR+)
  • Shortfall = synthetic shares created by uncovered short selling

AS THE INFORMED INVESTOR — YOUR COUNTERMOVES

None of the mechanisms above can be eliminated by a retail investor. But they can be recognised, timed around, and — for issuers — partially disclosed. The list below is practical, not theoretical.

Don't be the exit liquidity
When a broker accumulates anonymously and then closes a placement, the placement buyers fund the pre-placement clients' exit. Identify the placement agent and assume they accumulated before announcement. Size your own entry around that fact.
Map hold-period calendars
Every private placement and flow-through has a hold expiry (usually 4 months). Mark these dates. Expect selling pressure in the 2–4 weeks around expiry. Don't fight it — time your buying into the distribution wave.
Watch broker role switching
A broker flipping from consistent net buyer to consistent net seller is a major signal. Distribution has begun. Either they're unwinding client gains or the placement failed to find permanent homes.
Push issuers to buy InfoSuite
TSX InfoSuite Premium (~$1k/month) identifies the broker behind anonymous trades. Most junior boards don't know it exists. Ask IR to subscribe and disclose the monthly Anonymous Broker Summary. This is standard in Australia — rare in Canada.
Remove shares from the lendable pool
Move long-term holdings from margin to a cash account or registered position (TFSA/RRSP). This removes them from your broker's lendable pool — they can no longer be lent to short sellers betting against your own position.
Commission a position imbalance study
If anonymous selling persists without explanation, an issuer can commission a Generation IACP analysis. ~$15–30K cost is trivial vs. capital at risk if naked shorts are confirmed at PNPN-scale.
Use Alpha for large passive orders
Large passive limit orders on TSX can be leapfrogged by same-price broker-preferenced orders. Alpha has no broker preferencing — pure price-time. Route patient orders there when your platform allows.
Treat L1 data as insufficient
Broker summary data on free sites is delayed, aggregated, and stripped of anonymous / SME / dark markers. It is a useful signal — not a reliable record. Layer Level 2 depth, multi-venue volume, and insider filings before committing real capital.
